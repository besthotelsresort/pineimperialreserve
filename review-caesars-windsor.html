I grew up in this city, so let me tell you the thing the brochures never quite land: the best view at Caesars Windsor isn't inside the building at all. It's out the window. You stand at the glass of a high river-view room and the entire Detroit skyline is laid out across the water like a stage set someone built just for you, and at night it lights up and you cannot look away. I've checked into a lot of resort casinos in ten years of doing this job, and very few of them hand you a view that good for free.

What Caesars gets right, it gets right at scale. This is a big, confident riverfront resort, and after three separate stays I've come to trust its bones. Check-in, on a good night, is quick and the staff are genuinely warm — one long-time guest told me it was his third visit and it "never disappoints," and I've felt that same steadiness myself. Rooms are large and, more often than not, spotless; my river-view suite stayed cool through a brutal August and quiet enough to forget there was a gaming floor humming somewhere below.

The floor, the stage and Nero's

The casino is the engine of the place — a hundred thousand square feet of it, a poker room that never closes, and enough acreage that more than one guest has admitted it's genuinely hard to navigate. That's the honest catch of a room this size: the energy is fantastic if you want it, and a bit of a maze if you don't. My advice, as someone who reviews these floors with a stopwatch, is to learn one landmark early and orient off it.

The Colosseum is the reason a lot of people book at all, and as a former sound man I can tell you it's a properly good 5,000-seat room. I caught a comedy headliner there and had dinner at Nero's Steakhouse the same night — the pairing a lot of guests rave about — and both delivered. Where the resort dips is the everyday dining: the buffet, on my visit and in plenty of recent reviews, ran limited and a notch below what a place with this many restaurants should manage. Eat at the marquee venues; treat the buffet as a backup.

The service asterisks worth knowing

I won't gloss over the friction, because it's real and it clusters in predictable places. A number of guests — and I'd believe every one of them — describe check-in queues stretching past half an hour on busy nights, and at least one ran into a surprise pool fee that nobody had flagged. The bigger recurring gripe is housekeeping: limited service, no room service on some stays, and, in the worst account I read, a room cleaned only once across three nights with fresh towels only if you scheduled them. I didn't hit that myself, but it shows up often enough that I'd confirm your housekeeping schedule at check-in and ask about any resort fees before you hand over a card.

The verdict

Caesars Windsor is a resort that runs on spectacle — the skyline, the stage, the sheer scale of the floor — and when it's firing, it's one of the most entertaining stays in Southern Ontario. The rooms are big, the views are a genuine gift, and the headline dining and shows earn their reputation. Go in knowing the buffet underwhelms and the housekeeping can be thin, sort out the fees up front, and you'll get exactly what you came for: a lively riverfront weekend with a quiet, cool room to retreat to when you've had enough noise. Turn it up, then turn it off — on your terms.
